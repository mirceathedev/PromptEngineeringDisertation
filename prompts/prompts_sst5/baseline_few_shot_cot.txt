────────────────────────────────────────
CORE GUIDANCE
────────────────────────────────────────

Judge reviewer intent, not isolated words.

Movie reviews often judge acting, directing, writing, pacing, structure, originality, atmosphere, emotion, humor, visuals, entertainment value, and execution.

Film-review language is often indirect:
- “routine”, “thin”, “clumsy”, “flat”, “amateurish”, “generic”, or “bland” usually imply criticism.
- “with a flourish”, “immediacy”, “absorbing”, “compelling”, “well-shaped”, or “finely crafted” usually imply praise.
- tension, darkness, discomfort, or fear can be praise when they describe effective cinematic impact.
- sarcasm, metaphor, and comparison can reverse surface meaning.
- expectation-breaking can be praise when it implies freshness or originality.

First decide polarity.
Then decide intensity.

Use VERY only when the review is strongly evaluative, broad, memorable, one-sided, or focused on major craft/impact.

Do not over-upgrade ordinary praise.
