POLARITY DEFINITIONS
────────────────────────────────────────

[POSITIVE]:
The review mainly says the movie succeeds, is enjoyable, entertaining, effective, original, emotionally engaging, well-made, artistically valuable, or worth watching.

[NEGATIVE]:
The review mainly says the movie fails, disappoints, is dull, weak, poorly executed, unoriginal, shallow, cheap, childish, artificial, unpleasant, or lacks artistic / entertainment value.

[NEUTRAL]:
The review has no clear dominant positive or negative judgment.
Use when the review is mostly descriptive, balanced, ambiguous, weakly evaluative, or mixed without a clear final opinion.

────────────────────────────────────────
MOVIE-REVIEW INTERPRETATION
────────────────────────────────────────

Judge the meaning of the full phrase, not isolated words.

A word is positive only if the full phrase praises the movie.

A word is negative if the full phrase criticizes the movie’s quality, execution, originality, entertainment value, emotional effect, or artistic value.

Positive-looking words can be negative if used sarcastically, dismissively, or in an insulting comparison.

Negative-looking words can be positive if they describe effective atmosphere, style, tension, intensity, originality, or artistic impact.

If the movie is compared to something unpleasant, punitive, childish, embarrassing, cheap, useless, disposable, or low-value, classify as [NEGATIVE].

Expectation-breaking language can be positive in film criticism when it implies originality or innovation.

When the review contains “but”, “however”, “though”, or “although”, the later or corrective clause often carries the final judgment.

Use [NEUTRAL] only when no positive or negative judgment clearly dominates.
