LABEL DEFINITIONS
────────────────────────────────────────

[VERY POSITIVE]:
Very strong positive sentiment, strong praise, exceptional approval, strong recommendation, or unusually high appreciation.

[POSITIVE]:
Clear positive sentiment, approval, enjoyment, or mild/moderate praise.

[NEUTRAL]:
No dominant sentiment, balanced sentiment, mixed sentiment, uncertain sentiment, or mostly descriptive content.

[NEGATIVE]:
Clear negative sentiment, criticism, disappointment, or mild/moderate disapproval.

[VERY NEGATIVE]:
Very strong negative sentiment, strong criticism, rejection, dismissal, severe disappointment, or strong condemnation.

────────────────────────────────────────
FILM-REVIEW DOMAIN KNOWLEDGE
────────────────────────────────────────

Movie reviews often express sentiment indirectly.

Do not classify only from isolated words.
Classify from what the full phrase means as a judgment of the movie.

A phrase is POSITIVE when it suggests the movie succeeds in:
- acting or performances
- writing, story, or dialogue
- direction or execution
- pacing, tone, or structure
- originality or freshness
- atmosphere, style, visuals, or music
- emotional impact
- entertainment value
- artistic value

A phrase is NEGATIVE when it suggests the movie fails through:
- weak execution
- dullness or boredom
- lack of originality
- lack of coherence
- lack of emotional value
- poor acting, writing, or direction
- wasted potential
- cheapness, artificiality, or amateurishness
- shallow or childish treatment
- lack of purpose or value

────────────────────────────────────────
SPECIAL REVIEW LANGUAGE
────────────────────────────────────────

Before deciding sentiment, check whether the review uses special critic language.

1. Semantic inversion:
In film criticism, breaking expectations or conventions can be positive if it suggests originality or innovation.

If a review says the movie contradicts expectations, defies convention, subverts formulas, breaks norms, or is unlike ordinary movies, treat this as POSITIVE unless the sentence clearly criticizes poor execution.

Semantic inversion alone usually means [POSITIVE], not [VERY POSITIVE].

2. Genre/style language:
Words related to darkness, violence, bleakness, discomfort, strangeness, disturbance, or intensity are not automatically negative.
They can be positive when they describe effective atmosphere, style, tension, or impact.

3. Irony/metaphor:
Positive-looking words can be negative when used sarcastically, dismissively, or in an insulting comparison.
Always ask: what is the movie being compared to?

If the comparison makes the movie seem cheap, childish, punitive, embarrassing, disposable, unpleasant, or low-value, treat it as negative.

4. Contrast:
When a review uses “but”, “however”, “though”, or “although”, the later or corrective clause often carries the final judgment.
